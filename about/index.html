Why I'm building this
The system was never designed for most artists.
Siby John

India has an estimated 60,000+ active music creators. True career management — the kind where an agency handles your live bookings, brand deals, and global distribution — covers fewer than 500 of them. Everyone else navigates the industry without a map.

That isn't a talent problem. It's a structural one. Labels and management companies don't develop artists from scratch anymore — they expect you to arrive with an existing catalogue, a measurable audience, and momentum already built. The infrastructure to build that independently, the information, the contacts, the playbook, hasn't existed for the Indian market. Most of what's online was written for the Western market or written to sell something.

The economics make it harder. A single finished song costs roughly ₹35,000 once you factor in studio time, mixing, mastering, and session musicians. A video adds another ₹15,000 to ₹75,000. Marketing on top of that. An artist releasing four songs a year is spending ₹2-3 lakh before they've made a rupee back. And the first festival slot pays ₹4,000 to ₹5,000 per musician — if you get one at all. More than half of working bands in India accept below that for exposure. That's the baseline most artists are starting from.

India is also the second largest streaming market in the world by volume, with 79% of all streams going to local Indian artists. The audience is there. The market is real. The problem is that the infrastructure to connect artists to that audience — outside of the 500 at the top — barely exists.

I spent several years working in creator and music marketing at YouTube India — running campaigns with Anuv Jain, Jasleen Royal, Jassie Gill, and others, working with major labels on platform activations, watching how the system actually surfaces music versus how artists assume it works. The gap between those two things was large. The artists I met who were genuinely building something had almost no reliable information about how the business functioned.

So I built this. The Radar tracks what's actually growing. The Reviewer Database hands every artist the same contacts that previously required knowing someone. The Playbook is the India-specific guide that should have existed. The Labels and Agencies directory tells you honestly who's signing, who to approach, and what stage you need to be at before you knock.

Everything here is free. The only thing I ask is that you use it.
